Discover the mysteries of space, stars, and galaxies

What is the Star Constellation?

The constellations you can see at night depend on the time of year. Earth orbits around the Sun once each year. Our view into space through the night sky changes as we orbit. So, the night sky looks slightly different each night because Earth is in a different spot in its orbit. The stars appear each night to move slightly west of where they were the night before. Your location on Earth also determines what stars and constellations you see, and how high they appear to rise in the sky. The Northern Hemisphere is always pointing in a different direction than the Southern Hemisphere. This means that stargazers in Australia, for example, get a slightly different view of the sky and can see a few different constellations than those in the United States.

Aries

Aries is a constellation in the Northern Hemisphere, resembling a ram, and is most visible in December.

Location: Aries lies between Pisces to the west and Taurus to the east, visible at latitudes ranging from +90° to −60°.

Appearance: This constellation is relatively faint, with its main stars forming a V-shape. The brightest star, Hamal, which means "sheep" in Arabic, is accompanied by other notable stars such as Sheratan, Mesarthim, and 41 Arietis.

Visibility: Best seen in December around 9 PM, Aries can be challenging to spot due to the absence of exceptionally bright stars.

History: The name Aries is derived from Latin, meaning "ram." In Greek mythology, it represents the ram with the golden fleece, and in ancient Egypt, it was associated with the god Amon, often depicted as a ram.

Other associations: Aries is also the first sign of the zodiac, covering the period from about March 21 to April 19.

Gemini

Gemini is a constellation in the northern sky, most visible during the winter months.

Location: Situated in the second quadrant of the northern hemisphere, Gemini lies between Taurus and Cancer, and can be seen at latitudes ranging from +90° to −60°.

Stars: The brightest stars in Gemini are Castor and Pollux, named after the Greek mythological twins. These stars are positioned close together in the sky, symbolizing the twins' eyes, with their feet hanging in the Milky Way.

Visibility: Gemini is best viewed in January, February, and March, and remains visible in the evening sky until around May.

Zodiac sign: In astrology, Gemini is the third sign of the zodiac, spanning from approximately May 21 to June 21.

Symbol: The traditional astronomical symbol for Gemini is (♊︎).

Related objects: The constellation also contains the isolated pulsar Geminga.

Cancer

Cancer constellation is a small, faint zodiac constellation named after the crab in Greek-Egyptian mythology.

Location: It is positioned between Gemini to the west and Leo to the east, with Lynx to the north, and Canis Minor and Hydra to the south.

Visibility: Visible at latitudes between +90° and −60°.

Best visibility: Cancer is most visible at 9 PM during the month of March.

Notable stars: Lambda Cancri: A blue-white spectroscopic binary star, faintly visible to the naked eye.

55 Cancri: A sun-like star, visible to the naked eye, located about 40 light-years from Earth.

8 Cancri: A white star, faintly visible under optimal viewing conditions.

AZ Cancri: An M-type flare star.

Notable objects: M44, the Beehive Cluster: An open cluster that appears as a blurry patch when observed without a telescope.

Messier 67: An open cluster estimated to be between 3.5 to 5 billion years old.

RX J086.3+1527: An object believed to emit strong gravitational waves.

Leo

The Leo constellation is a prominent and well-known zodiac constellation, named after the lion in Greek mythology.

Location: Leo is positioned between Cancer to the west and Virgo to the east, with the Lynx to the north and Hydra to the south.

Visibility: Visible at latitudes between +90° and −65°.

Best visibility: Leo is most visible at 9 PM during the month of April.

Notable stars: Regulus: The brightest star in Leo, a blue giant, often referred to as the "heart" of the lion.

Denebola: A white star located at the lion's tail, easily visible to the naked eye.

Algieba: A binary star system that appears as a single point of light but is actually made up of two stars.

Zosma: A star near the lion’s back, known for its distinctive yellow-orange color.

Notable objects: Messier 65: A spiral galaxy located within Leo, often studied for its distinctive shape.

Messier 66: Another spiral galaxy in Leo, part of the Leo Triplet, a group of three galaxies.

NGC 3628: A barred spiral galaxy, also part of the Leo Triplet, known for its edge-on view from Earth.

Libra

Libra constellation is a zodiac constellation representing the scales of balance and justice in Greek mythology.

Location: Libra lies between Virgo to the west and Scorpio to the east, with Centaurus to the south and the southern Milky Way nearby.

Visibility: It can be seen at latitudes from +65° to −90°.

Best visibility: Libra is best viewed at 9 PM in the month of April.

Notable stars: Zubenelgenubi: The brightest star in Libra, a binary star with a greenish tint, often referred to as the "southern claw" of the scorpion.

Zubeneschamali: A blue-white giant star, symbolizing the "northern claw" of the scorpion.

Beta Librae (Chakr): A faint star that forms part of the scales in Libra.

Gamma Librae: A yellow giant star located near the center of the constellation.

Notable objects: Messier 4: A globular cluster that appears as a faint, blurry spot without a telescope.

Messier 19: A globular cluster located at the southern edge of Libra, notable for its dense star concentration.

NGC 5897: A distant globular cluster, located near the border of Libra and the neighboring constellation Scorpius.

Scorpio

Scorpio is a prominent constellation in the Southern Hemisphere, symbolizing a scorpion, and is most visible during the summer months.

Location: Scorpio is positioned between Libra to the west and Sagittarius to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +40° to −90°.

Appearance: This constellation is fairly bright, with its main stars outlining the shape of a scorpion. The brightest star, Antares, is a red supergiant, often called the "heart" of the scorpion. Other significant stars include Shaula, Lesath, and Dschubba.

Visibility: Scorpio is best observed in July and August around 9 PM, making it one of the most noticeable constellations in the sky.

History: The name Scorpio comes from Latin, meaning "scorpion." In Greek mythology, it represents the giant scorpion sent to kill the hunter Orion. The constellation held importance in ancient cultures, including those of Mesopotamia and Egypt.

Other associations: Scorpio is the eighth sign of the zodiac, covering the period from approximately October 23 to November 21.

Capricorn

Capricornus is a prominent constellation in the Southern Hemisphere, symbolizing a sea-goat, and is most visible during the fall and winter seasons.

Location: Capricornus lies between Sagittarius to the west and Aquarius to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +60° to −90°.

Appearance: This constellation is relatively faint, with its stars outlining the shape of a goat with a fish's tail. The brightest star in Capricornus, Alpha Capricorni (or Algedi), is a binary star system. Other notable stars in the constellation include Beta Capricorni and Delta Capricorni.

Visibility: Capricornus is most easily observed in September and October around 9 PM. While it is visible in both hemispheres, it is most prominent in the Southern Hemisphere.

History: The name Capricornus is derived from the Latin term "horned goat." In Greek mythology, the constellation represents the god Pan, who transformed into a goat with a fish’s tail to escape the monster Typhon. The constellation has been significant in ancient civilizations, such as the Babylonians, who linked it to the god Ea, associated with wisdom and waters.

Other associations: Capricornus is the tenth sign of the zodiac, spanning from about December 22 to January 19. Astrologically, it is associated with qualities like discipline, responsibility, and ambition.

Sagittarius

Sagittarius is a prominent constellation in the Southern Hemisphere, symbolizing an archer, and is most visible during the summer months.

Location: Sagittarius lies between Scorpius to the west and Capricornus to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +55° to −90°.

Appearance: Sagittarius is a medium-sized constellation, easily recognized for its distinctive "Teapot" asterism, which represents the body and bow of the archer. The brightest star in Sagittarius is Epsilon Sagittarii, known as Kaus Australis. Other notable stars in the constellation include Alpha Sagittarii (or Rukbat) and Delta Sagittarii.

Visibility: Sagittarius is most visible in the late summer and early fall months, typically peaking around 9 PM in July. It is best observed from the Southern Hemisphere but is also visible in parts of the Northern Hemisphere.

History: The name Sagittarius comes from the Latin word for "archer," and the constellation has been depicted as a centaur or a human figure with a bow and arrow. In Greek mythology, it is often associated with the centaur Chiron, who was known for his wisdom and kindness. Sagittarius has been a part of the Western celestial tradition since the Babylonian era.

Other associations: Sagittarius is the ninth sign of the zodiac, spanning from approximately November 22 to December 21. Astrologically, it is linked to traits like adventure, optimism, and a love for freedom and exploration. The constellation is also home to the Center of the Milky Way Galaxy, located near the star Sagittarius A*.

Taurus

Taurus is a prominent constellation in the Northern Hemisphere, symbolizing a bull, and is most visible during the spring months.

Location: Taurus lies between Aries to the west and Gemini to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +90° to −65°.

Appearance: Taurus is a well-known constellation, easily recognizable for its V-shaped asterism, representing the bull's head and horns. The brightest star in Taurus is Aldebaran, a red giant that marks the bull's eye. Other notable stars in the constellation include Beta Tauri (or Elnath), which forms the tip of one horn, and Zeta Tauri, which represents the bull's other horn.

Visibility: Taurus is best visible in the Northern Hemisphere during the months of November to April, peaking in January. It can be seen at latitudes as low as 65°N, making it a prominent winter and spring constellation.

History: The name Taurus is derived from the Latin word for "bull." In Greek mythology, the constellation represents the bull that Zeus transformed into to abduct the Phoenician princess Europa. Taurus has been known since ancient times, and it was one of the 12 signs of the zodiac recognized by the Babylonians. The constellation also has significance in other cultures, such as in the ancient Egyptian tradition, where it was associated with the bull-god Apis.

Other associations: Taurus is the second sign of the zodiac, spanning from about April 20 to May 20. Astrologically, Taurus is associated with traits like determination, reliability, and a strong connection to the earth, symbolizing a love for beauty, material comforts, and stability.

Aquarius

Aquarius is a prominent constellation in the Southern Hemisphere, symbolizing the water-bearer, and is most visible during the winter months.

Location: Aquarius lies between Capricornus to the west and Pisces to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +65° to −90°.

Appearance: Aquarius is a large, but faint constellation. It does not have a distinctive shape, but its stars are often depicted as a water-bearer pouring water from an urn. The brightest star in Aquarius is Alpha Aquarii (or Sadalsuud), a yellow supergiant. Other notable stars in the constellation include Beta Aquarii (or Sadalmelik) and Gamma Aquarii (or Al Iklil).

Visibility: Aquarius is most visible in the Southern Hemisphere from December to February, with its peak visibility around 9 PM in late summer and early fall. It is also visible in the Northern Hemisphere, though it appears lower on the horizon.

History: The name Aquarius is derived from the Latin word for "water-bearer." In Greek mythology, the constellation is associated with Ganymede, a beautiful young man whom Zeus took to serve as the cupbearer of the gods. Aquarius has been known since ancient Babylonian times, where it was linked with the god Ea, the god of water, wisdom, and creation.

Other associations: Aquarius is the eleventh sign of the zodiac, spanning from about January 20 to February 18. Astrologically, Aquarius is associated with qualities like independence, originality, and humanitarianism. The constellation is also known for its proximity to the Great Square of Pegasus and the Sun's position during the winter solstice.

Pisces

Pisces is a prominent constellation in the Northern Hemisphere, symbolizing two fish, and is most visible during the winter months.

Location: Pisces lies between Aquarius to the west and Aries to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +90° to −65°.

Appearance: Pisces is a large but faint constellation, typically represented by two fish connected by a cord. The brightest star in Pisces is Alpha Piscium (or Alrescha), which marks the point where the two fish are tied together. Other notable stars include Beta Piscium and Gamma Piscium, although they are relatively dim.

Visibility: Pisces is most visible from the Northern Hemisphere from late fall through early winter, particularly in the months of November and December. It is best seen around 9 PM during these months and can be observed at latitudes between 90°N and 65°S.

History: The name Pisces comes from the Latin word for "fish." In Greek mythology, the constellation represents the two fish that were transformed from the goddess Aphrodite and her son Eros, who jumped into the Euphrates River to escape the monster Typhon. The fish were later placed among the stars to commemorate their escape. Pisces has been significant in ancient civilizations, including the Babylonians, who associated it with fertility and the goddess Atargatis.

Other associations: Pisces is the twelfth sign of the zodiac, spanning from about February 19 to March 20. Astrologically, Pisces is associated with qualities like empathy, intuition, and creativity. The constellation is also known for containing the circumpolar constellation of Aries and being located near the Vernal Equinox.

Virgo

Virgo is a prominent constellation in the Northern Hemisphere, symbolizing a maiden or virgin, and is most visible during the late summer and fall months.

Location: Virgo lies between Leo to the west and Libra to the east, and can be seen at latitudes from +80° to −80°.

Appearance: Virgo is a large and relatively bright constellation, often depicted as a woman holding a sheaf of wheat. The brightest star in Virgo is Spica, a blue giant, which represents the hand of the maiden holding the wheat. Other notable stars in the constellation include Alpha Virginis (Spica), Beta Virginis (or Zavijava), and Gamma Virginis (or Porrima).

Visibility: Virgo is most easily observed during the months of April to June in the evening sky, particularly around 9 PM. It is most prominent in the Northern Hemisphere, though it can also be seen in parts of the Southern Hemisphere.

History: The name Virgo is derived from the Latin word for "virgin." In Greek mythology, the constellation is often associated with Demeter, the goddess of agriculture, or Astrea, the goddess of justice. It has been recognized since ancient Babylonian times and was part of the zodiac. In ancient cultures, Virgo symbolized fertility and the harvest.

Other associations: Virgo is the sixth sign of the zodiac, spanning from about August 23 to September 22. Astrologically, Virgo is associated with traits like practicality, attention to detail, and service. The constellation also contains the Virgo Cluster, a large group of galaxies located about 50 million light years from Earth.
